Key Messaging Points for McKelvey

1No Policies — At All
We asked on 4/3. Follow-up on 4/8. Defense is "still looking into" whether meal, rest, overtime, and expense reimbursement policies existed during the class period. The inference is clear: there were no compliant written policies. Under Brinker and Donohue, the burden is squarely on them.
2Ferra Underpayments Are Confirmed
BCG computed overtime regular rate underpayments at the 1.5x method. The new pay data — with full PeriodStart/End/PayDate and every wage code broken out (KPI, Ancillaries Incentive, Bono Nocturnidad, Bono Navideño, etc.) — lets us price Ferra exposure precisely. These non-discretionary bonuses were excluded from the regular rate.
3Their Own Records Are a Mess
Two productions with incompatible ID systems. The same ID number refers to different employees across files. 9 class members were erroneously included. 2 duplicate pairs. If they can't track their own workforce, how confident is the court going to be in their compliance systems?
4The Data Gap Before March 2022
Defendant says no non-exempt employees existed before 3/10/22 — but several employees on the class list have hire dates going back to 2008. Either those employees were misclassified, or there's a missing universe of pre-2022 data. Both options are bad for defense.
5Individual Claims Create Double Exposure
This isn't just a class/PAGA case. Mr. Santamaria has individual claims with their own valuation. A class settlement that doesn't address the individual track leaves the defendant defending a second front. Global resolution is in everyone's interest.
6PAGA Survives Regardless
PAGA penalties are independent of the class exposure. Every meal, rest, and Ferra violation triggers PAGA penalty exposure for all aggrieved employees. The individual track adds further PAGA standing. Not arbitrable. Separate from class valuation.
